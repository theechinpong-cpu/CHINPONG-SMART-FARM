Summary of Integers

Integers form the backbone of secondary school mathematics, acting as the bridge between basic arithmetic and abstract algebra. They are defined as the set of positive integers, negative integers, and zero. Unlike natural numbers, integers allow us to represent concepts like debt, temperature below freezing, and directional movement. [Image of number line showing negative and positive integers]

Absolute Value |a| is the distance from zero on a number line and is always non-negative. Every integer 'a' has a unique opposite '-a'. Together, they balance to zero.

For addition, use the "Asset vs. Debt" model. If signs differ, subtract the smaller magnitude from the larger one and use the sign of the larger magnitude. For multiplication and division, the Golden Sign Rule applies: same signs result in a positive output, while opposite signs result in a negative output.

Always remember: "The Enemy of my Enemy is my Friend." This explains why negative times negative equals positive. Prioritizing the sign before calculating the magnitude is the secret to avoiding errors. By visualizing movement on a number line, you move from mere memorization to true mathematical understanding.
